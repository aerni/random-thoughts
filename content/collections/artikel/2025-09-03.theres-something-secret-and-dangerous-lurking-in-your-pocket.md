We accepted all of this somewhere between downloading our seventh weather app and agreeing to let a flashlight request access to our contacts. It happened gradually, then all at once, the way most catastrophes do.

**The Permission Paradox**
Every app wants something. Your location. Your microphone. Your photos from 2014 that you forgot existed. You tap "Allow" because the alternative involves reading a 47-page terms of service written in a font size designed to discourage reading. At some point you granted camera access to a parking meter app. You have no memory of this. The parking meter app does.

**The Attention Economy, Explained Badly**
Somewhere, in a building with exposed brick and kombucha on tap, a very smart person is being paid to figure out how to keep your thumb moving. They have graphs. The graphs go up. Every second you spend scrolling is a small victory for the graph. You are not the customer. You are the product. You are also, somehow, paying a monthly fee. This is fine.

**What We Got In Return**
In exchange for all of this, we received the ability to identify a song within three seconds, argue with strangers in real time, and look up whether a specific actor was in that one movie at 2am without having to wait until morning and accept the uncertainty. These are, objectively, extraordinary powers. We just did not read the fine print about what they cost.
