The idea existed. That part is certain. It had weight and direction and the specific clarity that only comes when you're not trying to think of anything in particular — in the shower, on a walk, somewhere between floors in an elevator.

**Where Ideas Go**
Neuroscience has several explanations for why thoughts disappear, most of which involve the words "working memory" and "encoding" and do nothing to make you feel better about the loss. The simpler explanation is that the brain is not a filing cabinet. It is more like a very busy person who will absolutely remember to pass on your message, except when it doesn't, which is often, and there is no accountability structure in place.

**The Grief of the Forgotten Thought**
There is a unique sadness to knowing you had something and that it is now gone. Not lost like keys — lost like a dream at 10am. The shape of the idea is sometimes visible: it was about a person, or a system, or the way something works that nobody talks about. The content has evacuated. You are left holding an empty box that you know contained something valuable. This is worse than having nothing at all.

**The Case for Writing Things Down**
Every person who has ever lost an idea has, at some point, resolved to carry a notebook. Most do not follow through. Of those who do, most write "good idea — follow up" and later have no memory of what the idea was, only that it was good and required following up on. The notebook is not the solution. The solution is to write the actual idea, immediately, in enough detail that future-you — who is tired and slightly resentful — can reconstruct what present-you knew in that bright, fleeting moment. Nobody does this consistently. The ideas keep disappearing. We keep being surprised.
