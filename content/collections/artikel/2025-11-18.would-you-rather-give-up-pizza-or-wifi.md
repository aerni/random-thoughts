The premise seems absurd until you sit with it for a moment. Then it becomes genuinely distressing. Then you realise you've been sitting with it for twenty minutes and the pasta is getting cold.

**The Case for Pizza**
Pizza requires no infrastructure. It arrives warm, it is shared without negotiation, and it has never once buffered. A pizza does not ask for your password. It does not suggest you upgrade to premium for the ad-free experience. Pizza is also, technically, a complete meal — bread, sauce, dairy, optional protein — assembled by someone who had to learn a skill and then use their hands. There is dignity in this. Wifi cannot make the same claim.

**The Case for Wifi**
Without wifi, you cannot order pizza. This point was raised immediately at the dinner party and has never been successfully refuted. Beyond logistics, wifi represents access — to work, to people, to the answer to every question you've ever had at 3am. It is infrastructure in the truest sense: invisible until it's gone, catastrophic when it is. Anyone who has sat in a hotel room with bad wifi knows the specific despair that follows. Pizza cannot cause that despair. But it also cannot fix it.

**The Real Answer**
The question is not about pizza or wifi. It is about what kind of person you are. Do you believe that presence and warmth and something tangible matter more than access and convenience? Or do you accept that the world now runs on invisible signals and to opt out is to disappear? There is no correct answer. There is only the answer you give, and what it says about you, and whether your friends will let you live it down.
